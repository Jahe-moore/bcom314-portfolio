Where My Reputation Aligns — and Where It Doesn't

Across the semester, the strongest alignment between my identity and my reputation was around analytical thinking, discipline, and the quality of my work. The main gap was not whether I contributed, but how visible that contribution felt to others in collaborative settings. I was consistently present and prepared, but feedback showed that I did not always make my thinking visible in the moment. Over time, my presentation delivery improved, and the broader pattern became clearer: my professional persona is strongest when I pair strong execution with more visible communication. This section draws on five data domains — Core Values Exploration, Behavioral Communication Research, Team Culture Findings, Growth Mindset, and Identity-Brand-Reputation — to explain where that pattern came from and where it is going.

How I Measured This

Self-Perception Inventory (SPI)

My baseline self-assessment. It captures how I describe myself, how I tend to communicate, and what kind of work environment I prefer.

Professional Reputation Survey (PRS)

Outside feedback from people who have worked with me academically or professionally. It shows how others actually experience my strengths and habits.

360° Team Evaluations

Peer feedback from my BCOM team about my collaboration, communication, and contribution patterns during the semester.

Instructor and Preceptor Feedback

Feedback tied most directly to BCOM performance standards — especially presentation delivery, professionalism, and communication credibility.

Together, these tools gave me a way to compare how I see myself, how others experience me, and how those patterns changed over the course of the semester.

Where My Reputation Aligns

My SPI described me as analytical, independent, and results-oriented — someone who builds credibility through task completion and measurable outcomes. My Core Values and Identity-Brand-Reputation data pointed in the same direction: analytical rigor, discipline, and strong execution were not just self-descriptions, but patterns that others consistently experienced in my work. PRS respondents rated my Competence, Conduct, and Core Values as clearly aligned with how I present myself, and instructor feedback confirmed that preparation and work quality were evident throughout the semester. The identity I hold internally is not out of step with how others experience my contributions.

Where the Gap Appears

The gap appeared less in competence than in communication visibility. My Behavioral Communication and Team Culture data suggested I often contributed through preparation and final outputs, but not always through visible real-time discussion — peer feedback noted that my contributions were more evident in finished work than in live group exchange. The Charisma Diagnostic placed me in the Warmth Zone with a Confidence Quotient of −2.27, noting that warmth without visible confidence can lead others to underestimate engagement. My SPI work environment preferences help explain that pattern: I tend to operate best in direct, structured environments, which supports strong independent execution but can make me slower to externalize unfinished thinking in group settings. In professional settings, strong execution alone is not always sufficient — credibility also forms through how consistently others can observe judgment in action.

Change Over Time

To chart my growth longitudinally over the semester, I focused on instructor-observed changes in delivery, credibility, and audience presence across the three formal presentations. Each round built on the last — moving from establishing baseline structure and professional presence, to stronger vocal and physical delivery, to earning a Meets evaluation in the final presentation with confirmed improvements in posture, eye contact, audience leadership, and overall credibility.

Cultivating My Professional Persona

Moving forward, my goal is not to change the core of my professional persona, but to express it more visibly. My Growth Mindset data mattered here because the most useful pattern was not just where gaps existed, but how feedback gave me a clearer method for improving them. I want to keep the strengths that already align with my reputation — analytical rigor, discipline, and strong execution — while making those strengths more consistently observable in collaborative settings. That means speaking earlier in meetings rather than waiting until my thinking is fully refined, making my thought process visible before work is polished, and continuing to strengthen concise, confident verbal delivery. In the analytics and information systems roles I am pursuing, technical skill matters, but so does communicative visibility. My next stage of professional growth is making those two dimensions reinforce each other more consistently.
